Overview

As an Astrophysics Researcher at Illinois Tech, I have been leading a project to examine the formation mechanisms and classification of Sub-Subgiant stars. I have analyzed stellar spectra of 98 systems to extract radial velocities and orbital parameters of each stellar source. To complement this observational data, I built Monte Carlo simulations of synthetic binary populations, with which I was able to directly compare the modeled distributions to the observed data.

I am currently writing this work into a publishable document, focusing on the interpretation of the observational data and statistical results. My analysis indicates that Sub-Subgiant stars are predominantly found in binary systems, reinforcing binarity as a fundamental characteristic of this stellar population. My efforts toward this publication include synthesizing these results within existing theoretical formation channels and developing a consistent interpretation of the observed orbital properties. This paper aims to present a statistically grounded classification of Sub-Subgiant systems and their dominant formation mechanisms.

Research Workflow

This project is organized as a single end-to-end workflow: converting spectroscopy into radial velocities, interpreting those velocities through synthetic population modeling, and testing formation hypotheses using distribution-level statistics. The sections below summarize the major pieces of that workflow, with representative figures from the manuscript draft included where they best illustrate the approach.

Radial Velocity Extraction (Broadening Functions)

The foundation of the analysis is a radial-velocity extraction pipeline built around broadening functions (BFs). For each system, the spectra are processed to compute BFs and classify their morphology (e.g., SB1, SB2, or asymmetric profiles). These BF profiles are then fit to extract radial-velocity measurements across epochs, with uncertainty estimates derived through resampling methods (bootstrap) to provide realistic error bars for each measurement.

This approach produces a consistent set of RV curves across the sample and enables direct, quantitative measures of variability. In addition to the epoch-by-epoch velocities, semi-amplitude-style metrics (e.g., K estimates from extrema in the measured RVs) are computed as a compact representation of each system’s RV variability. Those derived distributions become the key connection point between the observed sample and the synthetic populations tested later in the workflow.

Population Modeling (Monte Carlo Synthetic Binaries)

To interpret the observational RV variability, I constructed Monte Carlo simulations of synthetic binary populations. Rather than comparing “true” orbital parameters directly, the simulations are designed to reproduce what the observations would have measured under realistic cadence limitations. Each synthetic population begins with a candidate underlying period distribution (e.g., field-like vs short-period or tidally synchronized scenarios) and then draws the additional orbital and geometric parameters needed to generate RV curves.

A critical step is forward-modeling the observing cadence: synthetic orbits are sampled in clustered “bursts” that mimic the multi-night observational structure common in real campaigns. This makes the comparison fair; because sparse sampling can systematically underestimate a system’s true semi-amplitude, the simulations explicitly capture those effects. The result is a set of synthetic, observation-matched semi-amplitude distributions that can be compared directly to the measured distribution from the SSG sample.

Results (CDF Comparison & K–S Statistical Testing)

The core results are presented as distribution-level comparisons between observed and simulated semi-amplitude behavior. For each candidate population model, the synthetic semi-amplitudes are converted into cumulative distribution functions (CDFs), which are then compared against the measured CDF from the observational sample. This format makes it easy to see which models reproduce the overall structure of the data and which deviate systematically (for example, by producing too many low-variability systems or failing to generate the observed high-amplitude tail).

To move beyond qualitative agreement, I apply two-sample Kolmogorov–Smirnov (K–S) testing to quantify consistency between each modeled distribution and the observed sample. Together, the CDF overlays and K–S results provide a statistical basis for evaluating proposed formation scenarios. In the current manuscript, this analysis supports the interpretation that Sub-Subgiants are predominantly binaries and that short-period / synchronized scenarios best reproduce the observed variability, while broader field-like period distributions are less consistent with the data.

Orbital Fitting (The Joker) — next phase

A parallel track of the project is orbital inference using The Joker, which provides a Bayesian framework for fitting orbital solutions to RV time series. This component is being developed as a continuation of the RV extraction work, but it is not yet included in the current manuscript draft. The motivation is to move from semi-amplitude distributions to full posterior constraints on parameters such as period, eccentricity, and semi-amplitude for systems with sufficient temporal coverage.

In practice, this involves defining priors appropriate for Sub-Subgiant binaries, sampling orbital posteriors from each RV dataset, and validating solutions through diagnostics and posterior predictive checks. As this work matures, it will enable per-system orbital solutions alongside the population-level conclusions already supported by the CDF and K–S analysis, and it will provide a direct bridge from “confirmed binarity” to well-characterized orbital architectures.

Publication (Writing & Interpretation)

The current phase of the project is focused on consolidating the pipeline, simulations, and statistical conclusions into a publishable manuscript. This includes writing the methods in a reproducible way (so that the BF extraction and uncertainty model are clearly defined), presenting the population modeling with explicit cadence effects, and organizing the results around hypothesis-driven distribution tests rather than isolated case studies.

The discussion work is where the observational constraints are connected back to formation theory. Here, the goal is to present a coherent interpretation of what the RV variability and distribution-level statistics imply about the Sub-Subgiant population—specifically, that binarity is a defining characteristic and that the data are most consistent with short-period / synchronized scenarios. This manuscript is being prepared as a first-author publication and is intended to serve as both a classification result and a statistically grounded constraint on dominant formation pathways.

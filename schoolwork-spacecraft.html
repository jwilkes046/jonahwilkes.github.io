Spacecraft Dynamics — Mission to Jupiter (CAPRICORN)

Mission design Interplanetary transfer Retrograde insertion Multi-target flybys Python simulation Technical report

The goal of this project was to design a mission trajectory that visits ten of Jupiter's moons in a single mission: Three outer moons (which my group chose to be moons in retrograde, as these are of scientific interest), the four Galilean moons, and three inner moons. hroughout the semester, we developed and applied spacecraft dynamics concepts—ranging from interplanetary transfers to moon-targeting maneuvers—to construct a physically consistent and executable mission. My work focused on shaping and optimizing the overall trajectory to accommodate both prograde and retrograde targets while remaining within mission constraints, particularly total Δv limits and minimizing time spent within Jupiter’s intense radiation belts (defined here as within 1,000,000 km of the planet).

Jupiter Mission Deep Dive

Mission Goal

Design a single spacecraft trajectory that visits a set of outer retrograde irregular moons, then transitions inward to reach the Galilean moons and selected inner moons. The mission is structured so the high-value outer retrograde targets come first, and the inner system encounters follow after a controlled alignment/slowdown.

Trajectory Phases

  • Earth → Jupiter transfer: start with an interplanetary transfer selected using a porkchop-style search.
  • Retrograde insertion: enter Jupiter’s system on a geometry that supports retrograde outer-moon flybys.
  • Outer retrograde flybys: execute a dual flyby sequence and then the final outer retrograde encounter.
  • Inner system sequence: perform a slowdown/alignment step, then proceed moon-to-moon with transfers and small adjustments.

Methods Used

This project forced me to move beyond “textbook” orbital mechanics and into practical mission design. I used Hohmann-style transfers as a baseline tool, then relied on Lambert solutions when simple transfers could not achieve the required intercept geometry within realistic time/∆v windows. The combination of propagation, targeting, and visualization made the trajectory design process feel like an engineering workflow rather than isolated homework problems.

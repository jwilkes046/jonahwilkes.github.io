Spacecraft Dynamics — Mission to Jupiter (CAPRICORN)

Mission design Interplanetary transfer Retrograde insertion Multi-target flybys Python simulation Technical report

The goal of this project was to design a mission trajectory that visits ten of Jupiter’s moons in a single mission: three outer moons (chosen to be retrograde irregulars due to their scientific interest), the four Galilean moons, and three inner moons. Throughout the semester, we developed and applied spacecraft dynamics concepts—ranging from interplanetary transfers to moon-targeting maneuvers—to construct a physically consistent and executable mission. My work focused on shaping and optimizing the overall trajectory to accommodate both prograde and retrograde targets while remaining within mission constraints, particularly total Δv limits and minimizing time spent within Jupiter’s intense radiation belts (defined here as within 1,000,000 km of the planet).

Trajectory Visualization

This is the full simulated trajectory animation produced from my Python simulation. At the time, the plotting package I used did not make it easy to style the spacecraft separately, so the spacecraft marker blends in with the moon markers. The mission sequence is best followed by watching the trajectory changes between flybys and transfers.

The animation begins with insertion from the Earth–Jupiter transfer into a wide, retrograde orbit, allowing the spacecraft to reduce relative velocity before its first targeting correction. The probe then executes a dual flyby of the retrograde irregular moons Pasiphae and Carme, followed by a retargeting maneuver to intercept Ananke.

After the outer-moon sequence, the trajectory transitions to an alignment point that enables Hohmann-like transfers within the inner Jovian system. Using Lambert-based targeting solutions, the spacecraft performs sequential flybys of the Galilean moons—Callisto, Ganymede, Europa, and Io—working from the outer system inward.

Following the Galilean encounters, the probe continues inward to fly by the inner moons Thebe, Amalthea, and Adrastea. After completing the final encounter, the spacecraft expends its remaining thrust and is designed to decay into Jupiter’s atmosphere, formally concluding the CAPRICORN mission.

Mission Architecture

The CAPRICORN mission was built as a step-by-step trajectory, starting with an Earth–Jupiter transfer and a retrograde insertion into the Jovian system. From this wide initial orbit, the spacecraft first targets the outer retrograde irregular moons, then gradually works inward to the Galilean and inner moons using retargeted, Hohmann-like transfers and Lambert-based solutions. Structuring the mission this way made it possible to reach high-value retrograde targets while staying within realistic Δv limits and reducing time spent in Jupiter’s most intense radiation regions.

What this taught me

This project moved beyond solving standalone orbital mechanics problems and into the challenges of full mission design. I learned how to translate two-body dynamics into a practical trajectory plan by selecting appropriate transfer methods, designing encounter geometries that enable sequential targeting, tracking Δv and timing tradeoffs, and validating the full mission through numerical simulation and visualization—where each maneuver has to work in service of the entire sequence.

Spacecraft Design — Mission to Titan (TITANIC)

PDR documentation Earth–Saturn transfer Aerobraking / aerocapture Relay constellation Surface lake operations Propulsion sizing ADCS

The goal of this project was to develop a complete mission concept and carry it through the Preliminary Design Review (PDR) stage. Over the semester we expanded beyond the orbital dynamics work from the previous course and into systems-level mission design—communications (spacecraft-to-spacecraft and to Earth), propulsion, spacecraft configuration, thermal/material considerations, and how these subsystems drive one another. This final project was the culmination of that progression, where our team demonstrated an end-to-end mission design that could be defended at PDR.

My work focused on the mission trajectory and timeline, propulsion sizing (including Δv and propellant mass), and initial ADCS feasibility for entry and on-orbit pointing, while also contributing across subsystems and keeping the overall mission concept consistent. Our final design delivers a semi-submersible surface vehicle to Titan’s Kraken Mare using a carrier/orbiter system, and returns data through deployed relay satellites to maintain continuous (or near-continuous) communications during lake operations.

Mission concept (high level)

The mission begins with the launch and insertion into an Earth-Saturn interplanetary transfer selected using porkchop plot analysis. Upon arrival, the transfer craft targets Titan and enters the atmosphere on an intercept trajectory which enables aerobraking, reducing transfer velocity while capturing into the moon's orbit. After orbit circularization, two relay satellites are deployed into phased orbits to establish constant communication coverage. With the relay satellites in place, hte carrier guides the surface vehicle through descent and splashdown at Kraken Mare, after which surface science operations can occur with data transmitted through the orbiters back to Earth.

Key design parameters from the PDR

  • Primary surface target: Kraken Mare (~68°N, 310°W), selected for its size, persistence, and science return.
  • Relay architecture: two deployed relay satellites phased to support continuous or near-continuous communications during surface operations.
  • Orbit baseline: circularized Titan orbit at ~10,000 km prior to satellite deployment and descent sequencing.
  • Timeline anchors: launch in May 2028, Titan atmospheric entry in mid-2034, and mission completion in late 2034, with these phases driving subsystem and operations planning.
